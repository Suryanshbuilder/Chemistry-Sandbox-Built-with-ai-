⚗ Chemistry Fundamentals

How Atoms Bond & React

Every substance in the universe — from the salt on your table to the water in your cells — exists because atoms decided to link up. Here's exactly how that happens, and what it looks like when they rearrange.

01
Ionic Bonding
The "Give & Take"

Some atoms are hoarders. Others are desperately trying to offload electrons. An ionic bond is what happens when these two personality types find each other — one atom completely hands over one or more electrons to another. Once the transfer is done, both atoms carry an electric charge (now called ions), and opposite charges attract — locking them together like tiny magnets.

Ionic Bond · Example
Sodium + Chlorine → Salt
The classic give-and-take

Sodium has one lonely outer electron it desperately wants to shed. Chlorine has seven outer electrons and needs just one more for a full shell. When they meet, Na hands its electron directly to Cl — no sharing, no negotiation.

Suddenly, sodium has a +1 charge and chlorine has a −1 charge. Opposites attract, they snap together, and you get NaCl — table salt.

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻  |  Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻
💡
The Rule of 8 (Octet Rule): Atoms are happiest when their outermost shell holds exactly 8 electrons — just like the noble gases. Ionic bonding is simply atoms doing whatever it takes to reach that magic number, even if it means fully surrendering an electron.
02
Covalent Bonding
The "Perfect Partnership"

Not every atom is willing to give up electrons entirely. When two atoms are similarly matched, they strike a deal: share the electrons instead. Both atoms overlap their outer shells, and the shared electrons zip back and forth between them — belonging to both nuclei simultaneously. The result is a bond that's often even stronger than an ionic one.

Covalent Bond · Example
Hydrogen + Oxygen → Water
Mutual sharing for mutual stability

Oxygen needs 2 more electrons to fill its outer shell. Each hydrogen atom needs just 1. Rather than stealing, they overlap their outer shells and share the electrons equally — each shared pair counts toward both atoms' totals.

Oxygen gets credit for 2 shared pairs (satisfying its need for 2), and each hydrogen gets credit for 1 pair (satisfying its need for 1). Everyone's shell is full. That's a water molecule.

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
💡
Ionic vs. Covalent at a glance: Ionic bonds form between a metal and a non-metal (electron transfer). Covalent bonds form between two non-metals (electron sharing). The electronegativity difference between the atoms determines which type wins.
03
The 4 Core Reaction Types
How bonds break and reform

A chemical reaction is just atoms breaking old bonds and forming new ones. The atoms themselves never disappear — they simply swap partners. Every reaction in chemistry, no matter how complex, can be traced back to one of these four fundamental patterns.

✨
Synthesis
A + B → AB

Two or more separate elements or compounds join together to build a single, more complex product. Think of it as two solo acts forming a band.

Real world: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl (making salt)
💥
Decomposition
AB → A + B

The exact reverse of synthesis — a single compound breaks apart into two or more simpler substances. Usually requires energy input (heat, light, or electricity).

Real world: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrolysis of water)
🔀
Single Replacement
A + BC → AC + B

A lone element muscles into an existing compound and kicks out the less reactive element — like a more popular person cutting into a dance. Reactivity determines who wins.

Real world: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑
🔄
Double Replacement
AB + CD → AD + CB

Two compounds meet and completely swap partners — like a square dance where everyone switches. Common in precipitation reactions, where the new pairing creates an insoluble solid.

Real world: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃
⚖️
Conservation of Mass: In every single reaction above, atoms are never created or destroyed. Count the atoms on both sides of any equation — they always balance. This is Antoine Lavoisier's law, and it has held without a single exception for over 250 years.
